What you need to know

Global climate change is in large part driven by the use of fossil fuels which introduce greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. Many prominent academics and politicians have called for nuclear energy technologies as a central part of the solution to climate change. Civilian nuclear technologies are commonly linked with the national security state apparatus. The central question is whether investment in nuclear technology is a sustainable solution to the problem of global climate change.

What is this research about?

Economic and military dynamics are major contributers to the release of carbon emissions. Many governments, environmentalists, and activists are calling for civilian nuclear power as a part of the solution to global climate change. The assumptions are that investing in nuclear energy will decrease carbon emissions without decreasing the overall consumption of energy by society, and that further nuclear modernization will address the limitations of this technology (such as nuclear waste). Little attention has been given to the influence of civilian nuclear technology and militaristic nuclear weaponry in driving carbon emissions around the world. Both nuclear power plants and nuclear weapons involve complex industrial processes that require enormous amounts of fossil fuel, and they may interact in unique ways to influence a society's overall CO2 emissions.

What did the researcher find?

Although nuclear facilities produce relatively small volumes of carbon dioxide when in operation, nuclear energy has numerous environmental impacts. The extraction and fabrication of nuclear fuel, the construction and maintenance of nuclear facilities, and the management of nuclear waste require vast inputs of energy and toxic chemicals. In relation to carbon emissions, there is little reason to believe that building a nuclear power plant reduces how much energy is being consumed by society. The central argument of pro-nuclear claims is that the market for "clean" nuclear energy would result in a reduction in fossil fuel consumption. In this view, a larger proportion of the energy consumed by society would come from nuclear power. It is also possible that nuclear energy production may simply increase the overall amount of energy available for use without resulting in a reduction of fossil fuel consumption. In this alternative view, an investment in nuclear power may result in an overall increase in carbon emissions and fail to reduce the consumption of fossil fuels.
